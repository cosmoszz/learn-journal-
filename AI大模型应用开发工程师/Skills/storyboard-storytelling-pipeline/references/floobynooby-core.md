# FloobyNooby Core

This skill treats the local `floobynooby_comp1` text pack as the primary authority when that source is available.

Use these core ideas as non-negotiable defaults.

## 1. Planning Comes First

- Do not begin detailed shots before you know the poses, timing, acting intent, and why each beat matters.
- Thumbnail first.
- Rough first.
- Review first.
- Clean later.

Working principle:

`plan -> rough -> review -> revise -> clean`

## 2. Clarity Is The Highest Priority

- A storyboard has very little time and very little space.
- If the audience cannot instantly tell what matters, the board is failing.
- One strong focal point is usually better than many competing ideas.
- Clear staging beats decorative complexity.

Questions to ask:

1. What should the viewer look at first?
2. What should they understand by the end of the shot?
3. What changed?

## 3. Shot Progression Must Follow The Story Point

- There is no fixed correct progression like `wide -> medium -> close`.
- The right progression depends on what matters in that scene.
- A scene can start on an ECU if that is the real dramatic punch.
- A scene can stay wider if public-space pressure matters more than facial feeling.

Working principle:

`story emphasis decides shot emphasis`

## 4. Close-Ups Are High-Impact Currency

- Use close-ups when a reaction or emotional turn is especially important.
- Save them for beats with real weight.
- Do not fill the board with extreme angles and tight framing.
- Contrast is what gives impact.

Questions to ask:

1. Does this beat truly deserve the audience being this close?
2. If I spend a close-up here, what am I losing later?

## 5. Staging Carries Character And Story

- Body language comes before facial detail.
- Pose, silhouette, posture, and character placement all affect meaning.
- A better staging choice can completely change who owns the scene.
- Reaction ownership matters: the most valuable reaction may belong to someone other than the speaker.

## 6. Motivate Cuts

- A cut is artificial, so help the audience across it.
- Common motivators:
  - eyeline
  - action continuation
  - entering frame
  - exiting frame
  - new information that no longer fits the current shot
  - transfer of reaction ownership

Working principle:

`each cut should hand the viewer to the next shot`

## 7. Protect Continuity

- Watch screen direction.
- Watch the staging line / axis.
- Re-establish new direction clearly when changing it.
- In action scenes, confusion is failure.

Questions to ask:

1. Does the viewer know where people are?
2. Does the viewer know where the threat is coming from?
3. Does the viewer know who is moving toward what?

## 8. Think In Animatic Terms

- Rough boards should be read as a sequence, not as isolated images.
- Judge timing, breathing room, escalation, and payoff at the sequence level.
- Many good single panels fail when assembled.

Working principle:

`the sequence is the real unit of judgment`

## 9. Storyboarding Is Translation, Not Decoration

- The board artist is translating a story into visual experience.
- The job is not to show off isolated cool shots.
- The job is to clarify, intensify, and carry the writer's dramatic intent.

## Quick Diagnostic

If the work feels wrong, check these first:

1. Did you skip planning?
2. Did you spend impact too early?
3. Did the wrong character get the reaction shot?
4. Did you lose continuity?
5. Did you explain a weak board instead of fixing it?
